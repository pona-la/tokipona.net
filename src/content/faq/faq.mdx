## What is Toki Pona?

Toki Pona is a constructed language. That means that someone decided what words and grammar to put in it, unlike natural languages, such as English or Urdu. Toki Pona has one main gimmick: it only has 120 words. Beyond that, Toki Pona is not very different from any other language in capability.

The number of speakers of Toki Pona is in the thousands. Toki Pona speakers are proud to be part of a community that emphasizes art and expression of identity. Toki Pona is a living language, and it's evolving through daily usage.

## Is Toki Pona a real language?

This depends on the definition of "real language." Toki Pona is a full human language that can be and is used for communication, and this is close to how most linguists would define "real language." Many people who ask this question are asking if Toki Pona is a fictional language, like Dothraki, Na'vi, Klingon, or Elvish. Toki Pona does not fit into this category because it is not from a piece of fiction. It was created in reality to be spoken in reality, and is not associated with any fictional universe.

Toki Pona is a constructed language, though, and many consider constructed languages to be outside of the category of "real language." Any argument about this would be an argument over the definition of "real language," which is more of a buzzword than a useful category here.

## How does Toki Pona work with only 130 words?

Toki Pona is highly context-dependent. This means that Toki Pona employs many of the same methods of communication that other languages do; it just doesn't allow for disambiguation via the usage of a more specific word. All other methods still work.

Speakers must pay more attention to surrounding events, prior knowledge, and presupposition (which is information implied by a sentence), in places where speakers of other languages would use or request a more specific word. Speakers may also clarify using an additional sentence.

## How long does it take to learn Toki Pona?

Like all languages, the amount of time needed to learn Toki Pona depends a lot on the person. It's also hard to measure how long it takes people to reach certain levels of proficiency, which in turn is because it is really hard to measure Toki Pona proficiency. For example, there exist many speakers who do not identify as fluent who are more skilled than many people who do identify as fluent. Every proficient speaker will provide a different amount of time that it took them to reach different levels proficiency.

It will definitely not take a weekend or a week. It's a common misconception that it is possible to learn Toki Pona to proficiency in such a short period of time. It is reasonable to memorize the vocabulary and grammar within a week, but it's not reasonable to understand the use cases of each word within a week, or to be able to form sentences fluidly that others can understand.

## How many words are there in Toki Pona?

The figure you’ll hear most of the time is 120 words, but most speakers use somewhere around 130. Some use a few more, some use a few less. On occasion, you'll find a speaker who uses more words, but it's uncommon. 

## What are Toki Pona's limits?

There are no hard limits for what Toki Pona is and isn't able to communicate. It's a language, and the limits of Toki Pona are fluid and are regularly re-evaluated as Toki Pona speakers continue to speak it. 

Here we describe a few challenges that Toki Pona speakers may come across. 

### Numbers and equations

The numbering system that speakers of Toki Pona use on a daily basis only has the words for 0, 1, 2, 5, 20, and 100. All other numbers are formed through adding these numbers together. This can be fairly unwieldly.

Because it is unwieldly, Toki Pona naturally discourages using numbers unless it is necessary. Speakers will often use descriptors like 'many' or 'few,' or 'old' or 'young,' or round to the nearest 20 or 100 if giving quantities. When necessary, Toki Pona is able to communicate large numbers through extended numbering systems like [nasin nanpa pona](). Methods to communicate fractions and decimals are less established, but speakers still usually manage to convey what they need.  

Mathematic notation is often described to be its own kind of language. World languages have each developed conventional ways to verbalize mathematic notation. Toki Pona speakers do not yet share a community-wide consensus for the best way to go about this, but many proficient speakers are able to talk abot basic operations. The realm of advanced mathematics is a frontier Toki Pona speakers are yet to explore. 

However, just as many people in normal society are content with not using algebra in their everyday life, many in the Toki Pona community are content with simply avoiding numbers. jan Mija is a Toki Pona speaker living in Canada who studies and teaches mathematics at the graduate level. When speaking Toki Pona, she takes joy in seeing just how many numbers she can successfuly avoid.

### Joining midway  

Toki Pona conversations often get increasingly complex the longer they go on. Occasionally, speakers joining midway miss key background information that was shared near the beginning and find themselves very confused. Thrust into the middle of a particularly complex conversation, it may be difficult for the listener to even clearly identify the main subject of what is being discussed. 

For the same reason, speakers may feel that Toki Pona is more difficult to skim through or back-read.


## Can I propose changes to Toki Pona?

Toki Pona evolves like a living language, not like a group project. While you can propose changes, the changes that get adopted are more likely to be subtle and natural, and less likely to be intentional and based on a coinage of a single community member, or even a group. You can, of course, change the way you speak toki pona. Just keep in mind that the more you diverge from the way others speak, the harder it will be for them to understand you. Also keep in mind that sometimes new speakers propose changes to address a problem other speakers have already found a natural solution for. 

## What does “Toki Pona” even mean?

In Toki Pona, *toki* means 'language, communication, speech,' while *pona* means 'good, positive quality.' Together, they mean something like 'positive language' or 'positive communication.' 

When speaking Toki Pona, depending on the context, a speaker can use *toki pona* to refer to the language itself, or to good communication more generally. After your friend gives a speech, you encourage them by saying, "*sina toki pona!*", or "You spoke well!"


## Who is the authority on Toki Pona?

There is no grand authority that dictates how Toki Pona is spoken. Any changes that Toki Pona undergoes happen naturally and flow from community usage. In other words, Toki Pona is a living language. 
 
English, too, is a living language. The English word,“fish” means what it means because most English speakers understand it as such. Imagine that a team of biologists, discovering that "fish" isn't a single genetic classification, decide to make up several new English words to to replace it and tell everyone to stop saying "fish." Ultimately, English speakers are the ones who use the language, and if these new ideas don't catch on, those biologists are out of luck. 

It is true that Toki Pona was a static piece of art at one point. During the early days, the creator of Toki Pona was forward-thinking and encouraged the nascent community to take the formation of the language into its own hands. "Now the rest depends on you. Go enjoy yourself. Create, play, and be pona!" When a speaking community did emerge, it joyfully embraced this philosophy. 

Although the creator may post material under the monicker "Official," both she and community members understand "Official" to function uniquely like a personal signature for herself. Use of this monicker does not mean that her Toki Pona is any more official, real, true, correct, or than that of any other. She has stated before that interpreting the word as having something to do with a "prescribed way to speak Toki Pona" would "contradict the whole point" of the messages of "creative freedom and community rights" that she put out throughout her career, and that indeed any engagement with her books is strictly "voluntary."
